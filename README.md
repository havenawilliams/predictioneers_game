Welcome to my (first attempt at a) recreation of a very special tool in political science (DISCLOSURE: WHOSE ORIGINAL DOCUMENTATION IS ENTIRELY PUBLIC AND IN ACADEMIC JOURNALS). This is an open sourced version of The Predictioneer's Game, a computer program designed by Dr. Bruce Bueno de Mesquita that does something fascinating--it predicts the future of political events and, potentially, changes them. Whether examining the outcome of a treaty signing, predicting the power balance as a war plays out, or capturing the back room workings of business elite, The Predictioneer's Game has a way to not only describe these events, but tells you which players have the ability to dramatically change the future. The way it does this is by using game theory, the math of how humans interact, and a whole lot of code to automate hundreds of game theoretic interactions. And currently, my program (kinda) does that. How cool is that?

Here's the basics of how it works. Or, better yet, here's the basics of how the math behind it works, because that's the most magical part. Game theory is a branch of mathematics that very concretely defines how games are played, with games being anywhere people strategize against each other (board games, school yard antics, chess, war, etc.). But there's a bigger picture to what I mean by the "math of games"--game theory describes how games operate at a theoretical level. So game theory tells us the math of how checkers becomes a draw, the math of how exactly people find out who the imposter is, and the math of how generals split the odds on their opponents intentions in war. The Predictioneer's Game is one giant, messy project that takes the math of how people play games in turns and implements that on an industrial scale.

Why would you make a program like this? Well, think about the butterfly effect. The whole point of the butterfly effect is that small movements--say, the flapping of a butterfly's wings--can set off a chain reaction that leads to a hurricane. The same goes for political events. If we can accurately predict events in the past using The Predictioneer's Game, we can also predict events that will happen in the future. More importantly, we can also figure out who could change the future the most by running the game multiple times and seeing what would happen is just one or two people went into their negotiations just a little bit differently than we currently believe them to be. The track record for this type of program is phenomenal. 

This program's original creator, Bruce Bueno de Mesquita, believes that all large wars start as small wars with unforeseen consequences. And I believe that finding the right butterfly before the hurricane is simply a good way to see those consequences.
